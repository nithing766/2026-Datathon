01 ‚Ä¢ Executive Summary

The Geographic Concentration of Air Inequity

The United States faces a "Long Tail" environmental crisis: while 75% of counties enjoy clean air, a small cluster of "Risk Epicenters" faces exposure levels up to 50 times higher than the national average. This disparity is worsened by a "Data Paradox," where the highest-risk regions in the Midwest and Southwest often suffer from the lowest monitoring oversight, hiding the true scale of the disaster.

üéØ Project Goal
Identify zones of environmental injustice, provide evidence-based decision support for the Justice40 initiative, and ensure that clean air and monitoring information are accessible to every community.
02 ‚Ä¢ Key Questions We Investigated

A Framework for Justice

This report is built around three core questions to drive policy action.

Question 1
Distribution of Burden

How is unhealthy air distributed across the U.S., and is it concentrated in specific "heavy tail" regions?

Question 2
Source Identification

In the most burdened counties, which specific pollutants (e.g., Ozone vs. PM2.5) are the primary drivers of risk?

Question 3
Monitoring Integrity

Do counties with high reported risk have sufficient data to be legally compliant, or are there hidden "blindspots"?

Map Metric
Unhealthy Air Rate

Purpose: Standardizes risk across counties with different monitoring schedules. It answers: "On days when air was measured, how often was it unsafe?"

[Total Unhealthy Days] / [Days with AQI]

This metric identifies areas where pollution intensity is high, even if monitoring is infrequent. It prevents "data deserts" (areas with few monitoring days) from appearing artificially clean simply because they report fewer total days.

Part B Geographic Concentration of Inequality

üìä
Quantifying Inequality: Strategic Triage

Why focus on the Top 10%? Environmental burden follows a "Power Law." The worst-off counties are not just slightly worse than average; they face risk levels exponentially higher. In a world of finite resources, effective policy requires triage: focusing immediate Justice40 funding on this "Heavy Tail" (the 10%) yields the massive returns in public health equity.

Observation 1
The "Southern California Epicenter"
San Bernardino, Riverside, and Los Angeles have the highest air quality gap in the country. Their Relative Risk Index is over 33, meaning people living there face a health risk 33 times higher than the national average. This shows that these areas are the "center" of air pollution, where the situation is much more extreme than the rest of the U.S.
Observation 2
Regional Clusters & Border Spikes
Beyond California, extreme risk is concentrated in Southwest border counties like El Paso, TX (24.57) and Dona Ana, NM (18.93). The data shows that air quality risk is not spread evenly across the nation but is highly concentrated in specific Southwest corridors and major industrial hubs.

04 ‚Ä¢ Recommendations

From Insights to Action

The goal is not only to improve national averages, but to guarantee a minimum standard of clean air access and monitoring coverage for every community.

Pillar 1 ‚Ä¢ Clean Air Access
Target high-burden counties
Use the Relative Risk Index to prioritize interventions in "Hotspot" counties. Match interventions to pollutant profile: NO‚ÇÇ ‚Üí transportation; PM2.5 ‚Üí stationary sources; ozone ‚Üí regional coordination.
Pillar 2 ‚Ä¢ Information Access
Define "Data Justice" Standards
Policy Proposal: Establish Days with AQI ‚â• 274 (75% completeness) as a mandatory prerequisite for federal environmental grants. This incentivizes local governments to improve monitoring networks to access Justice40 funding.
Pillar 3 ‚Ä¢ Accountability
Dynamic Warning Systems
Move beyond simple annual averages. Implement a national "Air Accessibility Health Map" based on Exceedance Rates to provide real-time identification of regions where risk is escalating despite low total days.
